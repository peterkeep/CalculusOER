    Subsection 1.3.2 Limit Properties

    Theorem 1.3.5. Combinations of Limits.

    If \(f(x)\) and \(g(x)\) are two functions defined at \(x\)-values around, but maybe not at, \(x=a\) and \(\displaystyle \lim_{x\to a}f(x)\) and \(\displaystyle \lim_{x\to a}g(x)\) both exist, then we can evaluate limits of combinations of these functions.
    1. Sums: The limit of the sum of \(f(x)\) and \(g(x)\) is the sum of the limits of \(f(x)\) and \(g(x)\text{:}\)
      \begin{equation*} \lim_{x\to a}\left(f(x)+g(x)\right) = \lim_{x\to a} f(x) + \lim_{x\to a} g(x) \end{equation*}
    2. Differences: The limit of a difference of \(f(x)\) and \(g(x)\) is the difference of the limits of \(f(x)\) and \(g(x)\text{:}\)
      \begin{equation*} \lim_{x\to a} \left(f(x)-g(x)\right) = \lim_{x\to a} f(x) - \lim_{x\to a} g(x) \end{equation*}
    3. Coefficients: If \(k\) is some real number coefficient, then:
      \begin{equation*} \lim_{x\to a} kf(x) = k\lim_{x\to a} f(x) \end{equation*}
    4. Products: The limit of a product of \(f(x)\) and \(g(x)\) is the product of the limits of \(f(x)\) and \(g(x)\text{:}\)
      \begin{equation*} \lim_{x\to a} \left(f(x)\cdot g(x)\right) = \left(\lim_{x\to a}f(x)\right) \cdot \left(\lim_{x\to a}g(x)\right) \end{equation*}
    5. Quotients: The limit of a quotient of \(f(x)\) and \(g(x)\) is the quotient of the limits of \(f(x)\) and \(g(x)\) (provided that you do not divide by 0):
      \begin{equation*} \lim_{x\to a} \left(\frac{f(x)}{g(x)}\right) = \frac{\displaystyle \left(\lim_{x\to a} f(x)\right)}{\displaystyle \left(\lim_{x\to a}g(x)\right)} \hspace{1cm} (\text{if } \lim_{x\to a} g(x)\neq 0) \end{equation*}

    Theorem 1.3.6. Limits of Two Basic Functions.

    Let \(a\) be some real number.
    1. Limit of a Constant Function: If \(k\) is some real number constant, then:
      \begin{equation*} \lim_{x\to a} k = k \end{equation*}
    2. Limit of the Identity Function:
      \begin{equation*} \lim_{x\to a} x = a \end{equation*}
